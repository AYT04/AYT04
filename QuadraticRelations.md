## Roots

The roots of a quadratic equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ are the values of the variable that satisfy the equation. These roots can be found using methods like factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula. The sum of the roots is given by $-\frac{b}{a}$, and the product of the roots is $\frac{c}{a}$. The nature of the roots can be real or imaginary, and if the coefficients are real, the roots will either be both real or a pair of complex conjugates.

## Axis of Symmetry

The axis of symmetry is a vertical line that divides a parabola into two equal halves. For a quadratic equation in standard form $y = ax^2 + bx + c$, the axis of symmetry can be found using the formula $x = -\frac{b}{2a}$. When the quadratic equation is in vertex form $y = a(x-h)^2 + k$, the axis of symmetry is $x = h$.

## Discriminant

The discriminant $b^2 - 4ac$ determines the nature of the roots:
* If $b^2 - 4ac > 0$, there are two distinct real roots.
* If $b^2 - 4ac = 0$, there is one real root (a double root).
* If $b^2 - 4ac < 0$, there are two complex conjugate roots.

## Factoring

Factoring quadratic equations involves expressing the polynomial as a product of its linear factors. For a quadratic equation in the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, the goal is to rewrite it as $(x - k)(x - h)$, where $h$ and $k$ are the roots of the equation.

## Completing the Square

To complete the square for a quadratic equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$:
1. Ensure $a = 1$ (if not, divide by $a$).
2. Move $c$ to the right side.
3. Add and subtract $\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2$ to the left side.
4. Factor the left side into a perfect square trinomial.
5. Solve using the square root property.

## Intercepts

Quadratic intercepts refer to the points where the graph of the equation crosses the x-axis and y-axis. To find the x-intercepts, set the equation equal to zero and solve for x. The y-intercept is found by setting $x = 0$ and solving for y.

## Domain

The domain of a quadratic function is all real numbers, expressed in interval notation as $(-\infty, \infty)$.

## Transformations

Transformations of quadratic equations involve shifting, stretching, compressing, and reflecting the graph of the parent function $f(x) = x^2$. The vertex form $f(x) = a(x-h)^2 + k$ is useful for identifying the vertex of the parabola.

* Vertical shift: $f(x) = x^2 + k$
* Horizontal shift: $f(x) = (x - h)^2$
* Vertical stretch/compression: $f(x) = ax^2$
* Reflection: $f(x) = -x^2$

## Applications

Quadratic equations have numerous applications in physics, engineering, business, and geometry. They are used to model the path of projectiles, design structures, optimize pricing strategies, and calculate areas and dimensions.
